ETFs and why we use them

What is an ETF?

An Exchange-Traded Fund (ETF) is an investment fund that tracks an index, specific asset or basket of assets to which it is pegged. ETFs are bought and sold throughout the day like securities on the stock exchange.

ETFs cover many areas of the market including stock indexes, stock market sectors, commodities, currencies, bonds and even instruments that track the volatility of the stock market.

ETFs, like mutual funds, contain a pool of assets, but they work quite differently. Mutual funds have their net-asset value calculated at the end of each trading day, but the price of ETFs price varies with supply and demand during the regular trading session.

Why we use ETFs...

Although ETF s have been around for over 20 years their attractiveness has grown enormously over the past decade as they have advantages over mutual funds which include:

Lower Costs:

Annual management costs of ETFs are normally considerably lower than mutual funds. It is regularly shown that many mutual funds fail to beat benchmark indexes, however, effectively track these indexes highlights their potential appeal to investors. Why pay high fees for mutual funds that may fail to beat an index such as the S&P 500, ASX200 or FTSE100 when you can buy an ETF that effectively tracks the benchmark index with a very low management fee?

Diversification:

ETFs allow investment in a broad array of markets and allows investors to better manage their risk against adverse moves in the market and are able to buy as little as one share of an ETF.

Transparency:

Unlike mutual funds, indexed ETFs freely visible as they must by law disclose their full portfolios every day.

Tax Efficiency:

Due to low turnover and the way they are structured, ETFs are typically more tax efficient than comparable mutual funds.
